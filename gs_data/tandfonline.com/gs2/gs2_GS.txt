Facilitating Prompt Mental Health Care adoption in primary care: a qualitative study of experiences with external implementation support

ABSTRACT
Objective: Given the significant extent of mental health conditions like anxiety and depression, effective prevention and treatment offers are imperative. Prompt Mental Health Care (PMHC) is a service model offering low-threshold treatment for individuals with mild to moderate anxiety or depression, initial substance use, or sleep difficulties. While external implementation support can enhance the adoption and sustainability of such models, its role in PMHC remains underexplored. This study investigates the experiences of PMHC providers with external implementation support.

Method: A qualitative exploratory design was employed. Twenty-two managers and team members from nineteen PMHC teams across Norway participated in focus group interviews. Data were analysed using reflexive thematic analysis.

Results: Three main themes emerged: (1) Cultural and relational implementation support, fostering team cohesion and a shared identity. (2) Organisational implementation support, facilitating integration into municipal structures, the navigation of administrative challenges, and supporting model fidelity. (3) Operational implementation support, promoting treatment quality and efficiency.

Discussion: Overall, the results highlight that external implementation support is highly valued and perceived as critical for the successful and sustainable implementation of PMHC, underscoring the need for comprehensive support strategies to integrate evidence-based mental health models in primary healthcare effectively.

KEYWORDS: Mental health, implementation, depression, anxiety, Prompt Mental Health Care, primary healthcare

Introduction
Mental health conditions are widespread. For instance, depression is predicted to rank first in the global burden of diseases by 2030 (United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Citation2022). In Norway, anxiety and depression are among the most common sick leave diagnoses, representing, together with mild mental health conditions, 87% of the total sick leave due to mental health-related causes in 2015 (Norwegian Institute of Public Health, Citation2023). Given the significant extent of these conditions, effective prevention and treatment offers are imperative, both to minimise individual suffering and socioeconomic burden.

Prompt Mental Health Care (PMHC) provides evidence-based psychological treatment, primarily cognitive behavioural therapy and guided self-help, to people with mild to moderate anxiety and depression problems, initial substance abuse problems, or sleep difficulties. It is a low-threshold service that is easily accessible, does not require a referral, and is free of charge, providing prompt access and short, efficient treatment (Knapstad et al., Citation2020; Smith et al., Citation2016). PMHC is a Norwegian adaptation of the English Talking Therapies model (former Improving Access to Psychological Therapies) (Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health, Citation2014), aiming to improve the delivery of and access to evidence-based psychological therapies for common mental health problems like depression and anxiety (NHS England, Citation2025; Wakefield et al., Citation2021). In Norway, PMHC is organised at the primary care level and should complement, not replace, existing services (i.e. GPs, primary mental health and addiction services and referral-based specialist services). Prior to the implementation of PMHC, services for this target group varied across municipalities, and access to evidence-based treatment methods in primary care was limited (Knapstad et al., Citation2020; Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health, Citation2014). PMHC is offered as a mixed care model with individually adapted treatment where the degree of support goes from (1) educative/education-based/tuition-based courses, (2) guided self-help, (3) treatment groups, to (4) individual therapy (Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health, Citation2014). Compared to people who receive standard treatment, studies have shown that, on average, people who receive PMHC after six and 12 months had approximately 60% and 50% higher recovery rates (Knapstad et al., Citation2020; Myrtveit Sæther et al., Citation2020). The PMHC group scored, on average, 88% and 60% lower on depression and anxiety symptoms, respectively, after six months, compared to the group that received standard treatment (Knapstad et al., Citation2020). After 12 months, the PMHC group scored 67% and 58% lower on depression and anxiety symptoms than the group receiving standard treatment (Myrtveit Sæther et al., Citation2020). Studies from England also show the effectiveness of Talking Therapies in treating patients, with increased recovery and return to work for a large patient group, suggesting a potential to reduce the cost burden of welfare benefits (Wakefield et al., Citation2021). Furthermore, PMHC can be socioeconomically beneficial compared to treatment as usual (Smith et al., Citation2025).

One of the health policy goals in Norway is to increase the implementation of low-threshold services following the PMHC model (Norwegian Directorate of Health, Citation2021). In Norway, national authorities are responsible for giving national guidelines and recommendations, while municipalities are responsible for the organisation and delivery of primary healthcare services. To increase access to evidence-based treatment for adults with anxiety disorders and mild to moderate depression, the Norwegian Directorate of Health initiated a pilot project with 12 PMHC teams in 2012. The pilot project evaluation concluded, among other things, that the model achieved its objective of being a low-threshold service that increases access to treatment (Smith et al., Citation2016). Thus, from 2012, to stimulate further implementation of PMHC, a central implementation action from Norwegian health authorities was time-limited government subsidies provided over three years with a gradual reduction. In addition, they have provided external implementation support from statewide authorities, including the Norwegian Directorate of Health, the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health, and the Norwegian Association for Behavioural and Cognitive Therapies, to reduce implementation risks and strengthen model fidelity (Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health, Citation2014). An overview of the external implementation support provided to Norwegian PMHC teams is presented.

Implementing new and evidence-based treatment models can be strengthened through various support actions that contribute to effective and successful implementation (Bauer et al., Citation2015; Fixsen et al., Citation2013). However, knowledge about implementation theory is often sparse among providers and staff in health and social services (Engell, Citation2025a). Receiving external implementation support can thus reduce organisational barriers to implementation and increase the possibility of achieving desired goals. Core elements such as leadership, stakeholder engagement, and continuous evaluation have been identified as essential for successful implementation (Engell, Citation2025c). These elements can be critical for ensuring that evidence-based practices, such as PMHC, are effectively integrated and sustained in primary healthcare settings. By supplying leadership support, education and training, and strengthening professional confidence, implementation support can help prevent resistance to change among staff and within the organisation (Fixsen et al., Citation2013). Systematic implementation support can also contribute to improving model fidelity when implementing evidence-based practice (Hartveit et al., Citation2022).

Implementation, however, is multifaceted, complex, and interactive (Bauer et al., Citation2015; Ritchie et al., Citation2023). It is also context-dependent, implying that support and implementation strategies must adapt to the relevant treatment model and context (Waltz et al., Citation2019) because a lack of contextual adaptation is among the factors contributing to evidence-based practices failing to be implemented in real-world settings (Engell, Citation2025b). Contextual knowledge about implementation support for PMHC is lacking, and research is needed to gain knowledge vital to adapting support and implementation strategies for future PMHC implementation and scale-up. Hence, this study aimed to develop knowledge on the needs of PMHC service providers for external implementation support by exploring the following research question: What are the experiences of PMHC service providers in receiving external implementation support?

Materials and methods
A phenomenological–hermeneutical approach was chosen to explore how PMHC providers experienced the external implementation support. This framework aligns closely with the study’s aim of gaining insight into participants’ lived experiences while also interpreting how these experiences were shaped by organisational, relational, and contextual conditions. The phenomenological dimension enabled an in-depth exploration of how participants made sense of the support in their daily practice, whereas the hermeneutical orientation allowed us to interpret these accounts in light of broader implementation structures and meanings. This approach therefore supported an analysis that moved beyond descriptions of support activities to an understanding of how they were perceived, negotiated, and integrated within team cultures and organisational settings (Dahlberg & Dahlberg, Citation2020; Lindseth & Norberg, Citation2022). An exploratory qualitative design was considered the most appropriate given the limited prior research on external implementation support within PMHC. Little was known about how such support is received and enacted within Norwegian municipalities, and exploratory qualitative methods permit openness to unexpected findings, variation in perspectives, and emergent patterns. This design allowed participants to articulate experiences in their own terms and enabled an inductive, reflexive analytical process suited to developing new insights into a relatively underexplored aspect of PMHC implementation (Brinkmann & Kvale, Citation2014). We selected focus group interviews (FGI) rather than focus group discussions or individual interviews because the study sought to capture how PMHC providers collectively interpret and negotiate the role of external implementation support. FGIs allowed for a more structured exploration of key topics while still enabling group interaction, which generated insights into shared practices and team-level meaning-making (Barbour, Citation2018; Braun & Clarke, Citation2022; Given, Citation2023; Morgan, Citation2018). Individual interviews would have provided valuable personal accounts, but would not have produced the interactional data necessary to understand how experiences were shaped, validated, or contested within teams.

Study setting
In Norway, the PMHC is integrated into primary health care, which is managed by the country's 357 municipalities, each with a population ranging from 200 to 700,000 inhabitants. As of February 2025, approximately 98 municipalities and city districts in Norway offer PMHC (Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health, Citation2014).

The Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health, commissioned by the Norwegian Directorate of Health, is among the organisations that provide external implementation support for PMHC in Norway (Norwegian Directorate of Health, Citation2021). To enhance the knowledge base for developing and adapting their implementation support, the Norwegian Resource Center for Community Mental Health and the Norwegian Directorate of Health initiated and funded the research project ‘Implementation Support for Prompt Mental Health Care’, from which the current study originates. A significant portion of the content and results reported in this study have been published in a Norwegian-language report as part of the original project’s deliverables to the commissioning body.

The Norwegian PMHC teams have been offered several external implementation support actions, and an overview of the support offered before this study’s data collection is provided.

Recruitment
The first, second and last authors got access to a list of contact persons in Norwegian PMHC teams from the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health. After consulting with the project manager for PMHC in the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health, we approached a selection of 29 PMCH teams for recruitment. The teams were strategically selected to gain diversity in geographical location, municipality size, implementation stage, and team size. We sent emails to the teams’ contact persons requesting participants who had a managing or central role in the preparation and/or initial implementation stages of the PMHC team. Representatives from 23 PMHC teams responded to the request. This corresponds to a response rate of approximately 80%. It is unknown why the remaining 20% did not respond to the email requesting participation. In addition to not wanting to participate, invalid contact information, staff changes, or discontinued teams could have resulted in missing responses. From the 23 teams, representatives from 21 teams agreed to participate. Still, two did not have the opportunity to join any of the scheduled focus group times, leaving participants from 19 PMCH teams.

Informed consent was obtained from all individual participants included in the study. All participants received an information letter in the initial recruitment email and again in the notice for the focus group. The letter informed them about the study, their voluntary participation, and their rights. Consent to participate was based on the participants choosing to log on and join the digital focus groups. Additionally, their consent was orally confirmed at the beginning of the focus groups.

Participants and PMHC teams
The participants in this study were persons with central roles in the preparation and/or early implementation of PMCH-teams, such as project managers, heads of division, professional managers, PMHC managers, and PMHC staff. Clinical users/patients were not included in this study.

A total of 22 persons from 19 PMHC teams participated. Three teams were represented by two persons, one central in the team's preparation stage and one in the initial implementation stage. The other 16 teams were each represented by one person with experience in one or both stages. All participants had extensive experience from working within health and welfare services, most with at least ten years, and currently had a management or team member position with a central role in the PMHC team’s implementation activity. An overview of the positions or roles held by the participants is provided.

PMHC teams from all parts of Norway were represented. There were four or five teams from each of the southern, central, western, and mid regions of Norway, and one team from the northern region.

The PMHC teams represented were at different stages of implementation; three teams were in the preparation phase, five were in the initial implementation phase, and eleven were fully implemented. Two of the PMHC teams were organised as inter-municipal cooperation between two municipalities. Thus, among the 19 teams, 21 Norwegian municipalities were represented. The population size of the teams’ catchment areas varied, and the distribution of teams in this regard is shown.

Data collection
We collected data in focus group interviews (Brinkmann & Kvale, Citation2014; Malterud, Citation2012). Focus group interviews are suitable, among other things, for exploring the participants’ experiences, opinions, wishes, attitudes, feelings, and ideas on a given topic. The interaction in the focus group, with mutual influence between the participants, can contribute to a richer exploration of and insight into the participants’ experiences than in an individual interview (Brinkmann & Kvale, Citation2018). Further, group dynamics add value when the meaning of experiences is confirmed or denied by joint input and experiences (Krueger & Casey, Citation2014; Morgan, Citation2018).

We organised five focus groups, each with two to six participants. The participants did not receive any economic or other compensation for participating in the focus groups. The focus groups were organised according to participants’ availability, while we also sought geographical and organisational variation (mix of roles and implementation phase) within each group to elicit contrasting perspectives.

The first, second and last authors developed a semi-structured interview guide to guide the focus group interviews. Different implementation support may be relevant at various stages of PMHC teams’ operations. Bertram et al. (Citation2015) propose four implementation stages, which, concerning the current study, can be recapitulated in three phases: preparation (assessment, investigation, anchoring/decision and planning), initial implementation, and full implementation. Although PMHC teams can benefit from implementation support at all stages of implementation, it is likely that the need for external support is most significant during the preparation and initial implementation stages. The interview guide was thus set up to emphasise the preparation and initial implementation stages. The guide focused on the participants’ experiences with preparing and implementing PMHC, with external implementation support, and their thoughts on future needs for implementation support.

The first, second, and last authors conducted the focus groups in pairs, alternating the roles of moderator and assistant. They participated in five, one, and four focus groups, respectively. The focus group interviews were conducted digitally on Microsoft Teams from October 14th, 2022, to November 3rd, 2022. Each focus group lasted between 75 and 90 minutes, was audio recorded, and transcribed verbatim.

Data analysis
The transcripts from the five focus group interviews formed the data material for the analysis, which the first, second, and last authors carried out. A reflexive thematic analysis method was found appropriate as it, among other things, is suitable for identifying, analysing, and reporting patterns (themes) in the participants’ experiences and opinions (Braun & Clarke, Citation2022, Citation2023). Inspired by Braun and Clarke (Citation2022) six phases for reflexive thematic analysis, we performed the following analytical process: (1) The first, second, and last authors familiarised themselves with the data by reading the transcripts and discussing initial thoughts and ideas to identify preliminary themes. (2) First author coded all the transcripts based on the preliminary themes, and (3) reviewed and clustered codes into themes and subthemes. The analytical software Nvivo 1.7.2 was used for phases two and three. (4) The first, second, and last authors discussed, interpreted, and adjusted themes and subthemes in a series of analysis meetings. In these meetings, disagreements about thematic interpretations and coding were discussed until consensus was reached. (5) The first author revised the themes, compared them with the overall data material, and identified descriptive quotes connecting the results to the data material. (6) The first and last authors presented the results to employees from the PMHC project group at the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health, reflecting on the findings and giving input on possible implications and relevance for practice. Last, the first author wrote the article draft, and the last author contributed with several critical revisions. The second, third and fourth authors performed a critical revision before completion.

Results
We identified three main themes during the reflexive thematic analysis: (1) cultural and relational implementation support, (2) organisational implementation support, and (3) operational implementation support. There are obvious overlaps between the themes as several of the support actions highlighted by the participants could help to facilitate and enhance both relational, organisational, and operational aspects of PMHC. However, the cultural and relational implementation support stood out as superior in terms of significance to the participants. These support actions were also perceived to enhance the impact of both organisational implementation support and operational implementation support. The results are presented under the three main themes, and quotes from the participants substantiate the findings throughout.

Cultural and relational implementation support
This theme includes supporting actions that were experienced to foster culture, networks, and relationships. The participants highlighted implementation support promoting team culture and relationships within and across teams as fundamental. They particularly valued this type of implementation support for fostering a sense of unity and shared purpose among team members, facilitating support, confidence, motivation, learning, and team development.

Participants emphasised the significance of team-building actions, such as joint education and national network seminars, in creating a cohesive team culture. In addition, the involvement of multiple teams in educational gatherings and national network seminars was highlighted as vital, as the participants experienced that it facilitated the establishment of a broader ‘PMHC family’, creating a supportive network that extended beyond individual teams.

Those gatherings are really useful, both for hearing how others do things and for keeping this PMHC identity up. […] There is something about that PMHC identity that is nice. (PMHC team member/Participant 17/Focus group 4)

The culture of sharing experiences and learning from each other was deemed significant for all implementation phases. National network seminars and supervision groups were highlighted as vital in maintaining this culture, providing a community for teams to exchange knowledge and best practices. Physical encounters facilitated networks and relationships that made it easier to contact each other between gatherings, utilising knowledge and experience sharing to answer questions or solve problems as they occurred. Participants suggested establishing more frequent local/regional networks and thematic networks to enhance this type of support further.

The first thing that I met was the culture of sharing, and then the threshold is lowered to make contact and call around and actually ask if people can share a bit so that you get an understanding of what a course looks like, what a brochure looks like. […] So, that network gathering was really beneficial. (PMHC manager/Participant 15/Focus group 4)

Cultural and relational implementation support was also reported to influence the utilisation of organisational support and to facilitate operational enhancements of PMCH teams’ professional and clinical practice. It was experienced to foster professional confidence, which, together with team unity, interdependence, and a sense of team spirit, was perceived as vital to the participants as it empowered them to stand on the front line, claiming PMHC’s space in the primary healthcare and defend the implementation of a new, evidence-based practice that provides mental health treatment differently than traditional mental health services. However, several participants also described uncertainty regarding how strictly the PMHC model should be followed and how much adaptation was permissible within municipal constraints and organisational structures. Cultural and relational support was experienced to play an important role in navigating this uncertainty. Participants described that it could strengthen professional confidence and a shared understanding of the model’s intentions, enabling teams to discern which elements required strict adherence and where local adjustments were considered both feasible and necessary. Hence, the cultural and relational implementation support was also deemed significant for establishing and maintaining model fidelity.

The fact that we were a whole team that started an education together also did a lot to make us believe in this ourselves, yes, to be strengthened, because we encountered a lot of resistance. In fact, perhaps most of all, in the already established municipal mental health services, because we were seen as competitors there. (PMHC manager/Participant 12/Focus group 4)

Although the cultural and relational implementation support actions like joint education and networking events were highlighted as significant, several participants experienced that limited finances were a substantial barrier to participation. This especially applied to teams located far from the Norwegian capital, Oslo, where most events were arranged.

Financially, [municipality name] now has the knife at its throat and is in such a cost-saving mode that even I, who sit here as head of the department and am passionate about us going to these network gatherings, can't send a single person because we have no money. (PMHC manager/Participant 1/Focus group 1)

To ensure equal participation opportunities regardless of the PMHC teams’ location and their municipalities’ financial situation, the participants recommended governmental subsidies for travel and accommodation costs or organising regional events to minimise participants’ costs. Although this was seen as a second-rate solution as it would hamper relationship building, another suggested solution was enabling digital participation.

Organisational implementation support
This theme includes actions to support PMHC teams with organisational factors, such as framework conditions, management, resources, and processes in municipal governance and service delivery. External organisational support was experienced as significant for anchoring PMHC in the municipality organisation, as well as for promoting model fidelity. In addition, the governmental subsidies were perceived as crucial for administrative decisions regarding implementing PMHC teams.

Participants expressed that they valued the availability of external authorities like the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health for guidance and support during the preparation stage. Where within the healthcare service chain should the PMHC team be organised? Should PMHC be organised as an independent service or be an integrated part of one of their current services? What should the framework and objective for a PMHC team be? How should they organise and implement PMHC to facilitate effectiveness and model fidelity? These are examples of questions the participants experienced that they needed guidance on in the early stages of implementation. Being able to consult an external authority with competence on favourable ways of organising PMHC based on different demographic and municipal service contexts was thus highlighted as significant.

I had extensive contact with someone at the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health, and that was invaluable to us. […] What was essential for us was the assurance we got from them and their experience, for instance, regarding the number of full-time positions recommended for the team based on our municipality’s target group population. (PMHC project manager/Participant 6/Focus group 2)

This form of external support was perceived as vital in determining how to organise and implement the PMHC team in the participants’ respective municipalities. Furthermore, they were explicit about the significance of organisational implementation support to facilitate model fidelity and ensure they implemented a PMHC service, not just service and treatment as usual. Furthermore, the participants noted that PMHC teams are vulnerable to economic cuts or shutdowns during budget reallocations or service reorganisations. External implementation support with guidance on how to prevent such scenarios was experienced as essential.

We are very exposed, and I am sure we will be on the municipality’s cut list. If we survive this year, we will be on the cut list for next year, and it will never end. (PMHC team member/Participant 4/Focus group 2)

Although politically and publicly controversial, as it limits municipal self-government, some participants suggested that the central government should earmark municipal funding to ensure that PMHC teams are not on the annual budget cut list. Some also recommended including municipal employees as a specific target group for PMHC services, as some participants had experienced that their positive feedback could influence decision-makers.

We also had an offer for employees in [name] municipality, and we noticed that our patients who worked in the municipality started to speak highly of us, both to the administration and politicians. There, we got a bit of help. (PMHC team member/Participant 18/Focus group 5)

Participants highlighted the vulnerability of PMHC teams during unforeseen societal events and municipal changes. An example reported was that during the process where two neighbouring municipalities merged into one municipality, the service reorganisation did not favour the PMHC team, i.e. by positions being rearranged, reporting lines became unclear, and the PMHC team was reorganised into another unit where they experienced less anchoring and a different expectation to the target group and service delivery. Another example was that during the COVID-19 pandemic, some teams faced closure reviews due to budget reallocation of funds to emergency health services. The participants experienced that getting ad hoc support, i.e. with knowledge and advice on strategic anchoring or participation in meetings with local management or politicians, from authorities like the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health in these situations was invaluable to help them prevent the PMHC team from being shut down.

*The pandemic [Covid-19] came when we were in the process of starting. So I had to go to my municipal director and our political representatives and give a presentation where I really had to fight for PMHC to continue despite the pandemic and the reallocation of funds. […] Sure, it was the support from [name of an employee at the Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health] that kept the ball rolling. It was on my initiative that I contacted him, and he had the time and opportunity, right? However, I think his help was crucial for us not to be cut off and for them to spend the money on the pandemic instead.* (PMHC project manager/Participant 6/Focus group 2)

Participants also expressed the need for more external organisational implementation support than what was already offered. For example, they considered that standardised information materials to support communication with municipal decision-makers could have promoted and impacted their implementation work. Further, they highlighted that a unified data monitoring and national registration system was desired to facilitate consistent data collection, reporting, and research on PMHC. According to their perception, such a system would significantly impact the implementation of PMHC locally and nationally, as well as address standardisation versus freedom to make local adaptations and thus facilitate model fidelity. Furthermore, the participants argued that standardised registration could be used for quality assurance, as well as for getting statistics and figures for municipal management, decision-makers, and politicians.

For those of us who are politically governed, it is incredibly important for us to be able to show figures, right? […] Being able to show that in the future you actually save money by spending time now, right, because the budgets are here and now, they are not ten years from now. So, having some numbers, preferably based on research, so that it’s not just guesswork, or, yes, that is the one thing I think is significant. (PMHC project manager/Participant 6/Focus group 2)

Operational implementation support
This theme includes actions that promote competence building and provide access to professional PMHC materials, clinical templates and tools. These support actions were perceived to enhance clinical skills and facilitate quality treatment. In addition, they could impact resource consumption and promote effectiveness, reducing the need for teams to use time to create their own materials. Access to operational implementation support was perceived as significant for maintaining treatment quality and model fidelity.

As described under the Cultural and Relational Implementation Support theme, actions like joint education and national network seminars seemed to facilitate team-building and enthusiasm for newly implemented PMHC teams, as well as establishing and maintaining the PMHC identity. In addition, the national network seminars were also highlighted as crucial for professional awareness and increasing PMHC quality by sharing new knowledge and providing operational support, and the joint education program was reported to boost professional confidence and provide concrete treatment methods.

We found the network gatherings for all PMHC teams very useful, both because of all the professional content – they were really great conferences in a way – and because all the presentations were just so relevant and useful. We just sat with wide eyes and took notes, and we could go straight back and use them in our daily work because they were spot on. It was for those who worked in PMHC, so it was incredibly useful. (PMHC manager/Participant 2/Focus group 1)

Further, the participants experienced the need for some additional operational implementation support. Centrally developed standardised materials, especially during initial implementation, were requested. For example, standardised information materials for collaborating partners, GP offices, and hospitals could save time and effort. A PMHC introductory course with ready-made content was also desired to facilitate quality and save time. Other requested templates included forms for enquiries, admission models, and evaluation forms. Clear directives defining the PMHC target group and practical tips were also deemed necessary. The recently published PMHC handbook was expected to meet some of these information needs and support new teams, reducing initial challenges, saving time, and being a resource for maintaining national consistency.

Furthermore, several digitalised tools were perceived as needed but costly, and some participants expressed the need for instruction and funds for municipalities to implement such tools.

I think that with digitally assisted self-help, it should be more like ‘the municipalities have to!’ Maybe just when it came and people could buy it, there should be funds linked to it to implement it because now, when there are no funds anymore, you compete for the general budget, which is nothing. Now we are saving electricity, and the municipality is very poor, so every week, we are told there is no money for anything, but then we can’t develop. (PMHC team member/Participant 3/Focus group 3)

Examples of digital tools requested by participants were clinical self-help and feedback tools, medical record systems tailored to PMHC, and digital communication solutions that complied with confidentiality regulations for patient interactions. National guidelines in this area were desired as differences in digitalisation levels among teams were noted, with some having advanced tools while others faced budget constraints.

Discussion
This study explored managers’ and staff's experiences with external implementation support when initiating and implementing PMHC in primary health care. Overall, the results show that various external implementation actions are valued during the preparation and initial implementation stages, including cultural and relational, as well as organisational and operational support. This implies that implementation support may impact the establishment, anchoring, and start-up of new evidence-based treatment models in primary health care and contribute to model fidelity and service provision quality. Notably, the results underscore the significance of cultural and relational implementation support when initiating new evidence-based treatment models in primary health care. Hence, we will address this theme in the discussion before briefly deliberating on some of the requested organisational and operational implementation actions.

The significance of becoming part of the ‘PMHC family’
Participants consistently highlighted the importance of external implementation support that promoted culture and relationship building. Cultural and relational support, such as joint education and national network seminars, was highly valued and facilitated team development, support, security, motivation, and learning through the social community of PMHC. Fostering a sense of belonging to the ‘PMHC family’ and a culture of sharing helped manage implementation challenges, strengthen model fidelity, motivate team members, and facilitate the sustainability of the new treatment model. The finding that cultural and relational implementation support was perceived as particularly significant is largely consistent with existing implementation research. In their scoping review of determinant frameworks in implementation science, Nilsen and Bernhardsson (Citation2019) show that social relations and support are central to successful implementation outcomes. In addition, Metz et al. (Citation2022) highlight the importance of building trusting relationships, arguing that trust among team members and across service teams is essential for effectuating policies and scaling effective practices in healthcare. Such trust can foster a collaborative environment where team members feel valued and supported, increasing motivation and capability and ultimately enhancing the implementation process and outcomes.

When team members feel safe to communicate openly, it can enhance trust and collaboration, leading to higher engagement and participation in implementation activities (Metz et al., Citation2022). Results in the current study show that, for the PMHC teams, the cultural and relational implementation support helps unify the teams, provides a common purpose, and positively impacts implementation. This indicates that group-based implementation actions, such as joint education and national network gatherings, can foster the trust and relationships highlighted by Metz et al. (Citation2022) as essential for team members to share their experiences and learn from each other. Hence, ensuring access to cultural and relational implementation support can help maintain trust across PMHC teams, facilitating a common organisational culture with shared values, beliefs, and norms. Further, it can promote team cultures characterised by positive relations, support, and trust that enhance motivation, commitment, and resilience among team members, all of which are critical for successful implementation (Klaic et al., Citation2022; Nilsen & Bernhardsson, Citation2019).

However, the current study shows that limited team budgets and a sparse municipal economy can lead to participation in externally provided cultural and relational implementation support being de-prioritised by those working in teams far from the venues of joint education, national seminars, and other gatherings. Considering the significance of cultural and relational implementation support for the future implementation of PMHC, financial coverage for participation should be considered. In addition, other implementation support actions that minimise participation costs can be considered, such as regional and local education and networks, as well as the possibility of digital participation.

Support for management and organisational anchoring
The results of this study underline the importance of PMHC teams receiving external implementation support for management and organisational anchoring. Implying, in line with Engell (Citation2025a, Citation2025c), that knowledge about implementation theory is sparse among managers and staff in PMHC teams. In particular, the results show that organisational support during the preparation and initial implementation stages is perceived as vital for anchoring PMHC in municipalities, facilitating appropriate organisation, good management, and promoting model fidelity. The results also emphasise the need for organisational support to navigate the complexities of implementing a new evidence-based treatment model like PMHC in a municipal setting. Offering implementation support that promotes continuous anchoring work appeared essential for establishing and maintaining PMHC teams. This is supported by literature, which has identified organisational support and organisational context as key elements for implementing healthcare interventions (Klaic et al., Citation2022; Nilsen & Bernhardsson, Citation2019). Further, financial subsidies from authorities appear crucial for the initial establishment of PMHC teams. In this regard, it seems essential to facilitate strong anchoring and a suitable organisational context for new evidence-based treatment initiatives to prevent them from being shut down when grant periods end. Given the complex municipal or primary healthcare context and services’ vulnerability to economic cutbacks, the results from the current study note that supervision groups for managers can be an action to support anchoring work for budget decision-makers. Providing extra implementation support for managers aligns with current literature identifying strong leadership as a key facilitator for both hospital-based (Cowie et al., Citation2020) and primary care-based (Kordon et al., Citation2024) interventions’ sustainability. Hence, early management access to organisational implementation support can strengthen PMHC sustainability in primary care because it can provide PMHC managers with implementation knowledge vital to obtain long-term anchoring and funding.

Requesting standardised material and tools for operation
The results show that professional and clinical standards can be supported through PMHC education, national network seminars, and supervision groups. However, access to standardised PMHC materials, clinical tools, and templates is highly requested as they are considered beneficial for quality assurance and resource-saving. Developing and providing standardised material can unify the services nationally, providing citizens equal service and treatment regardless of where they live. Developed by the National Resource Centre for Community Mental Health, the PMHC handbook (Norwegian Resource Centre for Community Mental Health, Citation2022) is an example of an implementation support initiative that can meet some of these requests.

Digitalisation appeared important for streamlining PMHC teams’ work and developing their clinical treatment in line with societal development. Digitalisation is an essential but challenging area within mental health services (Berardi et al., Citation2024; Torous & Blease, Citation2024). For example, challenges regarding data protection regulations can prevent effective communication with patients, and costs can lead to differences across municipalities in the digitalisation of health services (Berardi et al., Citation2024; Gullslett et al., Citation2024). Hence, a national minimum standard for digital tools is requested to guide municipalities, but given the varying municipal economy, centrally administered and distributed digital tools may be more feasible. A unified data monitoring system is another desired operational implementation support action. The current study results indicate variations in data recording practices across PMHC teams, like which measures they register and how they are registered (tools and frequency), and where data are stored (patient journals or local spreadsheets). This variation in measurement and registration practice can lead to inconsistencies that limit comparability and quality improvement across municipalities. Perry et al. (Citation2019) confirm the need for additional external data infrastructure to supply population-level data to improve quality, as single practices often do not have the capacity to manage this independently. Implying that for the implementation of evidence-based treatment models in primary care, a national registration and data monitoring system could contribute to implementation, quality improvement, better decision-making, and usability across teams (Clark, Citation2018; Clark et al., Citation2018).

Furthermore, the current study shows that PMHC teams are potentially vulnerable to changes and financial austerity in the municipalities. They may be de-prioritised in limited municipal budgets in competition with other important health and welfare services. Hence, a centralised and transparent registration and data monitoring system for PMHC teams, similar to that of the NHS in United Kingdom, could offer stronger empirical evidence on effectiveness, increase public credibility, and calculate cost-effectiveness related to, among other things, reduced healthcare use and improved employment rates (Clark, Citation2018). Hence, such monitoring could be vital in providing knowledge to decision-makers to help them prioritise PMHC and facilitate further scaling of PMHC in accordance with Norwegian health policies.

Strengths and limitations of the study
The current study contributes to understanding the significance of external implementation support when implementing new evidence-based treatment models like PMHC in primary health care. However, the following strengths and limitations should be considered when interpreting the results and their implications for practice and policy:

A qualitative study sample of 22 participants from 19 PMHC teams, with diversity in geographical distribution and implementation stages, consolidates the results’ transfer value to implementing other evidence-based treatment models in primary health care. However, limitations should be considered for other cultural or organisational contexts.

The recruitment process relied on voluntary participation, which could have introduced self-selection bias. Participants who chose to engage in the study might have had mainly positive or negative experiences with external implementation support. At the same time, those with neutral or less pronounced views may not have had the same motivation to participate and thus have been underrepresented. Focus group interviews are beneficial for exploring collective experiences and generating rich data. However, the focus groups may have contributed to interactional dynamics that shape expressed viewpoints, including social desirability, consensus-seeking, or reluctance to voice more critical perspectives. While the moderators encouraged divergent views and probed for contrasting experiences, the possibility remains that some nuances or less favourable experiences were underrepresented. These considerations do not negate the findings, but highlight the need to interpret the strong positive orientation in light of potential recruitment and group-process influences.

The reflexive thematic analysis is inherently interpretative. Thus, it was a strength that more than one researcher participated in the analytical process. The three researchers who participated in the analysis have different professional, academic, and research backgrounds, which broadened their perspectives and minimised the personal influence of interpretations and results.

Implications for practice and research
The results of this study call attention to the significance of providing external implementation support that fosters a cultural and relational community when implementing new evidence-based treatment models like PMHC in primary healthcare. Among other things, support actions such as national network seminars, joint education, regional networks, supervision groups, and networks and supervision groups exclusively for managers should be considered. Further, the results imply that a range of organisational and operational support actions are perceived as vital to promote and sustain the implementation of such models. Support actions that can be considered include (but are not limited to) access to professional advice and guidance to facilitate anchoring and navigating the contextual complexities; financial support for travel and accommodation costs for attending education, seminars, and network gatherings; standardised professional material and tools; support and adapt digitalisation; and data monitoring with standardised registration categories for reporting and research.

This study points out several areas where further research is needed to enhance the implementation and effectiveness of evidence-based treatment models like PMHC in primary healthcare. The current study indicates that financial constraints and geographical location can limit access to external implementation support. Research is needed to examine the effects of these variations on the quality and sustainability of evidence-based treatment models like PMHC in primary healthcare. Investigating the effectiveness of regional versus national support actions and the potential benefits of financial subsidies for travel and accommodation could provide valuable insights. Participants also highlighted the need for standardised materials, clinical tools, and templates. Future studies could explore the impact of standardised materials on the consistency and quality of PMHC services across different municipalities. The current results show that the vulnerability of services like PMHC to budget cuts and organisational changes is a concern. Investigating strategies to enhance the long-term sustainability of evidence-based models like PMHC in primary healthcare is thus requisite in future research. Furthermore, research is needed to explore the feasibility and impact of implementing a unified data monitoring system, including the data types to be collected, the benefits for decision-making, and the potential challenges.

Investigating the relationship between different types of implementation support (cultural, organisational, operational) and model fidelity could provide deeper insights into effective implementation strategies. Among other things, digitalisation can increase the efficiency of external implementation support. However, the impact of digitalisation on cultural and relational aspects of implementation support needs further investigation, and future research should explore how digital seminars, network meetings, education, and supervision compare to physical gatherings in terms of fostering team culture, relationships, and professional confidence.

By addressing these areas, future research can contribute to the development of more effective and sustainable primary healthcare, ultimately improving access to evidence-based mental healthcare for adults with mild to moderate anxiety and depression.

Conclusion
This study shows that external implementation support is highly valued by PMHC providers and is perceived as significant for the successful implementation and sustainability of the PMHC model in primary healthcare. The findings underscore the importance of cultural and relational implementation support in enhancing team cohesion and professional confidence, which in turn supports organisational and operational aspects of implementation. External implementation support seems to enable PMHC managers and team members to better navigate the complexities of implementing an evidence-based treatment model in primary healthcare. Future implementation strategies should prioritise comprehensive support actions to ensure the effective and sustainable integration of evidence-based treatment models in primary healthcare.
